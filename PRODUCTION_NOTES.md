# Production Notes

This document was written by Greg Blotzer with substantial AI assistance from two Claude models across distinct sessions.

**Original design session (Claude Sonnet 4.6).** The architecture was developed iteratively through extended conversation with Sonnet 4.6 over approximately 25 hours of focused session time across 6 days. The working pattern was collaborative: I framed questions and pushed back when proposed designs had structural problems; Sonnet 4.6 generated, extended, and refined the architectural specifications. Several load-bearing design properties emerged from this back-and-forth rather than from either party's initial proposal — the aliasing-not-absorption rule for deduplication (Section 5.5.1) is one example, where the original framing treated deduplication as content absorption and the back-and-forth surfaced that this would create dangling pointers in reasoning chains. The resolution (aliasing with supersession edges) preserved chain integrity and became one of the document's load-bearing properties.

**Review and refinement session (Claude Opus 4.7).** Once the design was substantially complete, I started a separate session with Opus 4.7 to get a fresh-eyes review from a different model. The review surfaced several issues the original session had reinforced through familiarity — a precedence bug in the scoring function formula (Section 5.6.1), an under-specified normalization model lifecycle (Section 3.5.4), an echo-chamber failure mode in the corroboration semantics (Section 4.5), a security gap in the migration tier assignment (Section 10.3). Opus 4.7 also helped develop the cost model (Section 8), the executive summary, and the Semantica engagement in Related Work (Section 1.4) once that prior art surfaced. The corroboration distinction between independent rederivation and confirming echo, the systems-not-agents principle articulated in Section 1.3, and the migration review gate (Section 10.3.3) were all developed or sharpened during this session.

**What the AI assistance was and wasn't.** The judgment about which design choices to defend, which to revise, and which to discard rested with me throughout. The substantive contribution of both models was in surfacing options I would not have considered alone, in catching inconsistencies between sections, in helping articulate principles I held implicitly, and in pushing back when my framings were imprecise. The cases where the models proposed wrong answers and I caught them were as numerous as the cases where I proposed wrong framings and the models caught them. The working dynamic was genuinely collaborative rather than directed in either direction.

**Why this disclosure matters.** The HLD's subject matter is AI-assisted development at team scale. The document is itself an artifact of the practice it argues about. A reader is entitled to know that the work was produced with the tools it discusses, and to evaluate the design with that context in mind. Concealing the AI assistance would undermine the document's intellectual honesty; failing to characterize the assistance specifically would substitute vague disclaimer for substantive disclosure.
